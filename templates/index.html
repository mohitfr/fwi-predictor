Why daily assessment matters

Fire weather conditions can shift significantly within hours. A site that carried low risk at dawn may reach critical danger levels by midday as temperature rises, humidity drops, and wind picks up. Waiting on regional forecasts or end-of-day reports leaves a gap that this tool is built to close.

By taking readings at noon — when conditions are typically at their most severe — and running an immediate assessment, field teams can make informed decisions about patrol priorities, burn restrictions, and crew positioning before conditions escalate.

Danger level reference
Typical field conditions and required response at each rating level.
Low
FWI 0 – 5
Cool temperatures, high humidity, little wind. Fuels retain moisture. Fires unlikely to start and easy to control if they do.
Routine monitoring sufficient.
Moderate
FWI 5 – 12
Warm and drying conditions. Surface fuels beginning to dry. Fires can start and spread but are generally controllable with standard resources.
Increase patrol frequency in dry zones.
High
FWI 12 – 20
Hot, dry, and windy. Fine fuels critically dry. Fires spread rapidly and spotting ahead of the fire front is likely. Initial attack must be fast.
Restrict open burning. Brief field crews.
Very High
FWI 20+
Extreme heat, very low humidity, strong winds. Fires are erratic and difficult to control. Any ignition source is dangerous.
Issue public advisory. Pre-position response teams.
